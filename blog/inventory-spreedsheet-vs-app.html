Most systems start as a spreadsheet.

Google Sheets, Excel, Airtable — some version of a table with rows and columns.

Item name. Price. Platform. Status.

It’s usually set up in under an hour, and for a while, it works.

You can see everything in one place. You feel organized. There’s a sense that the system is “handled.”

And early on, it is.

Spreadsheets

Spreadsheets are flexible in a way most tools aren’t.

You can shape them however you want:

Add columns
Reorder fields
Track whatever feels important

Airtable adds a more visual layer. Excel handles larger datasets well. Google Sheets is easy to access from anywhere.

There’s no learning curve beyond what most people already know.

Where they tend to hold up

Lower inventory counts.

Consistent workflows.

When items don’t move around much.

In those situations, a spreadsheet can stay accurate for quite a while.

---

Where they tend to drift

Not all at once — gradually.

An item gets listed but not logged.

Something gets moved but the location isn’t updated.

A row is skipped because you’re in the middle of something else.

Nothing breaks immediately.

But over time, the sheet and the physical inventory stop matching exactly.

That gap is usually small at first.

Then noticeable.

Then frustrating.

---

Inventory apps

Inventory apps approach the problem differently.

Instead of a blank structure, they come with a defined system.

Fields are prebuilt. Workflows are assumed.

Some focus on listings. Some on analytics. Some on cross-posting.

Where they tend to help

Reducing manual entry in certain areas.

Keeping listing data consistent.

Providing visibility into sales and performance.

They’re often more structured than a spreadsheet, which can reduce certain types of errors.

---

Where they don’t always line up

Many are built around the idea of inventory as data first.

Not necessarily as physical objects in a space.

So while the information may be organized, the physical side still depends on whatever system the seller has created on their own.

Shelves, bins, piles, closets — that part usually lives outside the tool.

---

A smaller category: storage-focused tools

There’s a smaller group of tools that focus specifically on where items are stored.

Not listings. Not analytics. Just location.

These tend to be simpler by design.

Less about managing the business, more about making retrieval predictable.

Where that approach fits

Home-based setups.

Mixed storage (bins, closets, shelves).

Situations where items move frequently.

Or where the main friction point is finding something after it sells.

---

What actually holds up over time

Most systems don’t fail because they were poorly designed.

They drift because they rely on perfect consistency.

And most real workflows aren’t perfectly consistent.

Things move quickly. Items get shifted. Steps get skipped.

Over time, small gaps add up.

The systems that tend to hold up are the ones that stay aligned with how the inventory is actually stored and handled day to day.

Not just how it’s tracked.

---

What works on paper and what works in practice aren’t always the same thing.
